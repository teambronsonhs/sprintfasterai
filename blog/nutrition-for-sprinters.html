Nutrition for Sprinters

Sprinting is short and explosive, which means your nutrition needs look different to an endurance athlete's — even though most generic sports nutrition advice is written for them.

Bronson Hearn-Smith · 8 min read

A marathon runner needs to fuel hours of steady output. A sprinter needs to fuel a handful of maximal, explosive efforts and then recover well enough to do it again tomorrow. That's a different job, and it changes what "eating right" actually means for you.

The basics: what each macronutrient is doing

You don't need to track every gram to get this right, but it helps to know what each part of your plate is actually for.

  • Carbohydrates fuel the explosive efforts themselves and refill the glycogen stores your muscles use during sprints and gym sessions. Sprinters who under-eat carbs tend to feel flat and heavy-legged in the back half of a session.
  • Protein repairs the muscle damage from sprinting and lifting. Most athletes do well aiming for roughly 1.4-2.0g of protein per kilogram of bodyweight a day, spread across meals rather than loaded into one.
  • Fats support hormone health and should make up a reasonable share of your diet — they're not something to minimize purely because they're calorie-dense.

Before training

Aim for a meal with carbs and some protein roughly 2-3 hours before a session — something like chicken, rice and vegetables, or pasta with a lean protein. If you're training sooner than that, drop to a smaller, easily digested snack 30-60 minutes out: a banana, a small bowl of cereal, or toast with honey. The goal is showing up with fuel in the tank, not a heavy stomach.

During short sessions

For most track and gym sessions under 90 minutes, water is genuinely enough — sprint sessions are short and high-intensity rather than long and steady, so you're not draining energy stores the way an endurance session would. Save sports drinks for longer sessions or hot conditions where sweat losses are significant.

After training: the window that actually matters

The 1-2 hours after a hard session is when your body is most ready to use protein and carbs to repair and refuel. You don't need a complicated supplement stack — a simple meal with both covers it:

  • Chicken, rice and vegetables
  • Greek yoghurt with fruit and granola
  • A protein shake with a banana, if a full meal isn't immediately possible
  • Eggs on toast with a side of fruit
The best post-session meal is the realistic one you'll actually eat, not the perfect one you'll skip because it's complicated.

Race day or time-trial day

Competition day is the wrong time to try something new. Stick to meals and snacks you've already used successfully before training, eaten at a familiar time before you race. Keep hydration steady throughout the day rather than chugging water right before you compete, and have a simple carb-and-protein snack ready for between rounds if you're racing more than once.

Common mistakes

  • Under-eating to "stay lean." Sprinting rewards power, not low bodyweight for its own sake — chronic under-eating tends to cost you speed and recovery long before it changes how you look.
  • Skipping breakfast before morning sessions. Training fasted makes explosive sessions feel harder than they need to and can blunt the quality of your work.
  • Treating supplements as a shortcut. Consistent meals matter far more than any product; supplements are a small top-up at best, not a substitute.
