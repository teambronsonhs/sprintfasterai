Best Gym Exercises for Sprinters

Sprinting is decided by how much force you can put into the ground in almost no time at all. Here's how to train for that, specifically.

Bronson Hearn-Smith · 8 min read

A sprinter and a bodybuilder can lift the same exercises and get almost opposite results, because they're training for different qualities. Bodybuilding optimizes for muscle size under control, with long sets and short rest. Sprinting needs rate of force development — how much force you can produce almost instantly — which means heavier loads, lower reps, and far longer rest between sets.

Why "lifting big" isn't the goal

Strength matters, but only as a foundation. The actual transfer to sprinting comes from how quickly you can express that strength. A sprinter who squats a huge amount but moves slowly off the floor often runs slower than a sprinter with a smaller squat who moves the bar fast. That's why sprint-focused gym work always pairs heavy strength work with explosive, low-load power work — strength gives you the engine, power work teaches you to use it fast.

The exercises that matter most

Squats — back squat or front squat

The base strength exercise for sprinters. Heavy, low-rep squatting (think 3-5 reps) builds the raw leg strength everything else is built on. Front squats are a useful variation if you want to keep the torso more upright, which carries over well to sprint posture.

Hip thrusts and Romanian deadlifts

The glutes and hamstrings are the prime movers in sprinting, especially in the drive phase, and they're chronically undertrained by athletes who only squat and press. Hip thrusts directly train hip extension — the exact movement that drives you forward off the ground — and Romanian deadlifts build the hamstring strength that protects against the single most common sprint injury.

Olympic lift derivatives — power cleans, hang cleans, clean pulls

These movements train the body to produce a large amount of force very quickly, which is closer to what sprinting actually demands than a slow heavy lift. They take coaching to learn properly, so if you're new to them, start with lighter loads and prioritize the speed and technique of the pull over the weight on the bar.

Plyometrics — bounds, depth jumps, box jumps

Plyometric work trains your tendons and nervous system to absorb force and return it almost instantly — the same quality that makes a good sprinter's stride look "springy" rather than effortful. Keep volume low (these are demanding on the body) and quality high; a handful of maximal bounds beats twenty mediocre ones.

Core and anti-rotation work

A sprinter's core isn't there for show — it's the link that transfers force from your legs through your hips without leaking energy sideways. Planks, dead bugs and anti-rotation cable work matter more here than crunches.

A simple session structure

Most sprint-focused gym sessions follow the same shape: warm up and activate the hips and ankles, then move from the most explosive, technical work to the most fatiguing. A typical order is plyometrics first (while you're freshest and most coordinated), then Olympic lift derivatives, then your main heavy strength lift, then accessory and core work to finish.

Train the explosive stuff first, while you're fresh — fatigue is the enemy of the exact quality you're trying to build.

Fitting gym work around track sessions

The biggest planning mistake is heavy lifting the day before a max-speed track session, which leaves your legs flat exactly when you need them sharp. Where possible, put your hardest gym session on the same day as an easier track session (or a rest day), and keep gym work light or skip it entirely the day before your most important speed work.
